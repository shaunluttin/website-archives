Mussel Aquaculture Summary

 

Project Description

Opportunity

Bivalve mollusks “are the planet’s most environmentally sustainable source of animal protein” according to Richard Waite, World Resources Institute, and identified as a key component for increasing the world’s food supply with minimal environmental impact.  British Columbia (BC), with its large natural resource of microalgae in nutrient-rich coastal waters, offers fantastic conditions for bivalve production.  With Canada’s developed infrastructure, a very significant opportunity exists for the sustainable production, marketing and distribution of large quantities of quality protein from BC’s coastal waters.

Mussels have one of the highest flesh/shell ratios as well as the highest metabolic rate of bivalves, making them a first choice for protein production. In addition, mussels are grown suspended in the water column allowing the use of efficient and environmentally neutral farming practices. Bivalves eat naturally occurring phytoplankton and do not require any protein-based diet with additives that are usually used for finfish and almost all land-based farm animals.  No medications, pesticides or fertilizers are consumed or used.  Suspended culture does not influence beach ecology. 

In spite of these benefits, mussel production in BC is low.  Latest statistics available (2012) list production at 237 tonnes compared to 27,000 tonnes produced annually in eastern Canada and well over 100,000 tonnes in New Zealand.  Part of the reason for low BC production is the need for hatcheries to produce the mussel larvae planted on farms.  As a result the industry is relatively new compared to industries relying on natural mussel set.  However, analysis doesn’t show this as a significant economic restriction.  Instead, lack of knowledge of mussel biology, inadequate innovation of efficient practices specific to mussel culture and inadequate investment limits production. 

 Corporate Responsibility

Singular Biogenics Corporation (SBC) proposes the creation of a mussel aquaculture enterprise for social benefit.  Although SBC is currently structured as a for-profit company, profit isn’t the bottom line.  Instead, SBC envisions an alternative business structure, joining a movement that places ethics and the environment first.  Protein production for social benefit with very limited environmental impact coupled with ethical relations with staff and customers are its top priority.  However, seafood production is profitable and the enterprise will be economically self-sufficient and able to contribute to other efforts having the same priorities.

Requirements

In BC, mussel larvae are provided by land-based shellfish hatcheries and then grown for three months in land-based nurseries to reach outplanting size. Although existing hatcheries and nurseries can sell seed mussels large enough for outplanting, the three-month-old mussels are expensive, not set on proper substrate and not always available at the best time of year, if at all.  Partial reliance on external sources for seed mussels can be appropriate for the initial years of operation, but controlling the source through first owning and operating a nursery and then a hatchery is necessary for the long-term.  Initial production in the first two to four years can be supported by purchasing two-week-old larvae from existing hatcheries, establishing an innovative and efficient nursery and growing the larvae to outplanting size on proper substrate in the nursery.  Microalgae must be grown to feed the larvae while in the nursery.  SBC's high-density algae production technology now under development can produce the necessary algae.  The enterprise would acquire a hatchery and begin its own larvae production as soon as it becomes economically feasible.

Creation of a mussel enterprise requires acquisition of one or more farms or “deepwater growout leases” where mussels take 12 to 18 months to reach market size once transplanted to the farm from the nursery. Grow-out conditions are optimum in bays and inlets along the west coast of Vancouver Island where existing deepwater shellfish leases are available.  Suspension structures (longlines and floats) and work rafts are required on the grow-out lease although often are already in place.  A reliable boat is needed to transport harvested product from the farm to port and seed mussels from the port to the farm.

First year harvests would be conservative in order to develop production and marketing systems.   Product can be marketed fresh in western North America. When the fresh market reaches saturation, in at least ten years, additional production would be marketed as preserved product (smoked, frozen, canned) and would be distributed globally.

Management Team

SBC’s principal Matthew Salo, has extensive experience farming mussels in BC waters. As a founding partner in Island Sea Farms, Inc., producer of Salt Spring Island mussels, Salo built the first hatchery dedicated to mussels in North America and six farms in different areas along the BC coast. He has gained a valuable understanding of the potential and challenges of mussel farming in BC and familiar with different oceanographic conditions as they relate to mussel farming around Vancouver Island. His talents and experience include management skills, business acumen, communication and creative problem solving, particularly in regard to the marine biological environment. He has developed many innovative mussel culture techniques that are being used to advance BC mussel farming. Salo is dedicated to developing a social enterprise for food production that benefits tomorrow’s society.  See Salo's Bio on this website for more information.

Finances

A mussel aquaculture venture can be funded with $1.7 million over three years.  The money is used to lease or purchase a land-based or floating nursery for growing two week old mussel spat purchased at low cost from existing hatcheries.   This facility, or similar, would be used as a site for a future hatchery to supply spat once hatchery expenditures are justified.  An initial grow-out site on the west coast of Vancouver Island would be leased or purchased.  An initial fulltime staff of five plus part-time help is required.  Expenses for nursery equipment, boats, rafts and other farm equipment, utilities, small tools and equipment, patenting, legal and licensing fees and memberships bring the total investment to $1.7 million.  An additional $1 million would be required to develop a small hatchery, although these funds could come from sales revenue once the breakeven point is reached. First year production revenue is projected at $200,000.  Revenue would double each year for the first four years and then increase by 50% in subsequent years. Breakeven is expected during the fourth year of operation, sooner than normal for shellfish aquaculture ventures due to SBC’s experience.
